The SR latch, five layers down

An SR latch is the simplest memory there is: two cross-coupled gates that hold a single bit. This is the house part CEC3007, a Critical Error set-reset latch made of two NOR gates wired so each one drives the other. SET (S high) forces the output Q high and it stays high after S releases; RESET (R high) forces Q low and it stays; hold both low and the latch remembers its last value. Drive both high and the two gates fight, the forbidden state, a deliberate teaching hazard that settles to an undefined level when you release it. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the function table. The second is the flow network, bistability as a held level that feeds back to sustain itself. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves, two valves each held by the other. The fourth is the gate-level schematic, the two cross-coupled NOR gates powered from the supply rails with the feedback fully wired. The fifth is the silicon, a NOR cell laid out as its CMOS transistors with the rails tied.
