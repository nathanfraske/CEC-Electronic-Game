The JK / T flip-flop, five layers down

A JK flip-flop is a D flip-flop whose D input is computed from J, K, and the flop's own output. This is the house single-gate CEC3076, a positive-edge-triggered JK that no vendor sells alone, since the real JKs are all dual parts. On each rising edge of the clock, J and K choose the next state: hold when both are low, set when only J is high, reset when only K is high, and toggle when both are high. That toggle is what the feedback buys: routing the output back into the input removes the old SR latch forbidden state and lets the cell flip. Tie J and K together and it becomes a T flip-flop, the divide-by-2 cell at the heart of every counter and frequency divider; with T held high and the clock running, Q is exactly half the clock frequency. Internally it is the same master-slave pair of latches as a D flip-flop, with a small steering front-end ahead of it computing D = J·Q̅ + K̅·Q. The first tier is the logic symbol with the real pinout and a live timing diagram. The second is the flow network, the two latches as chambers with a steering manifold feeding the master. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the schematic, the transmission-gate master-slave with the JK steering gates and the Q feedback drawn in. The fifth is the silicon, the cross-coupled inverter cell that stores the bit.
