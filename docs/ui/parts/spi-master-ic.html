The SPI master, five layers down

Serial is a parallel word sent one bit at a time through a shift register. This is the house CEC5021, the master end of a four-wire Serial Peripheral Interface bus, and it owns the clock. A rising edge on START pulls CS low, then the master shifts its eight-bit word out on MOSI most-significant-bit first, one bit per SCLK pulse, while clocking the peripheral's reply in on MISO at the same time. Out and in on the same edges is what full-duplex buys; the master driving SCLK is what makes it synchronous, so there is no baud rate to agree on. After eight bits CS releases. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the live protocol timing diagram, the signature of a serial part. The second is the flow network, the shift register as a bucket brigade that marches the word out one stage per clock tick. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves, the clock advancing every stage by one. The fourth is the real device at the register-transfer level, the shift register as a chain of eight D flip-flops with a bit counter and the clock and framing control, all powered from the rails. The fifth is the silicon, one flip-flop of that register laid out as its CMOS transistors, the cell the whole register repeats.
