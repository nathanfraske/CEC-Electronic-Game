The Schmitt inverter, five layers down

A plain inverter flips its output the instant the input crosses one threshold, so a slow or noisy edge can make the output chatter back and forth. The Schmitt-trigger inverter fixes that with hysteresis: it has two thresholds, a positive-going trip VT+ and a lower negative-going trip VT-, and between them the output simply holds its last state. Here it is the SN74LVC1G14, a single Schmitt inverter in a five-pin SC70 package. It still computes Y = NOT A, but the input has to climb past VT+ to pull Y low and fall back below VT- to let Y go high again, so a wobble inside the band cannot flip the output. The first tier is the logic symbol with the real pinout and the hysteresis loop. The second is the flow network, the gate as plumbing with a drain that trips at a level set by the output through positive feedback. The third opens that mechanism as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the schematic, the six-transistor CMOS Schmitt where two feedback devices move the switching point. The fifth is the silicon. The deadband shown here is the gap between the two trips, the margin that makes the part shrug off noise.
