The SPI slave, five layers down

Serial is a parallel word sent one bit at a time through a shift register. This is the house CEC5022, the peripheral end of a four-wire Serial Peripheral Interface bus, and it has no clock of its own. The master selects it by pulling CS low and supplies the clock; on each rising edge of that incoming SCLK the slave samples MOSI into its receive register and at the same instant shifts its reply out on MISO, most-significant-bit first, so a read and a write happen together. After eight bits, once a full word has landed, RXVALID rises. That is the whole idea of speak only when clocked: every real SPI sensor, flash, or display is this, selected and clocked from outside. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the live protocol timing diagram, the signature of a serial part. The second is the flow network, the shift register as a bucket brigade the master's clock marches one stage per tick. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the real device at the register-transfer level, the shift register as a chain of eight D flip-flops driven by an SCLK edge detector, with a bit counter and the RXVALID and framing logic, all powered from the rails. The fifth is the silicon, one flip-flop of that register laid out as its CMOS transistors, the cell the whole register repeats.
