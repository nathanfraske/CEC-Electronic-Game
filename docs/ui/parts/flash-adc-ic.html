The flash ADC, five layers down

A flash converter turns a voltage into a number the fast way, in parallel. This is the house CEC1080, a 3-bit flash analog-to-digital converter and the densest part in the set. A reference ladder cuts VREF into seven evenly spaced levels; seven comparators each ask at once, is VIN above my level; the answer is a thermometer code, a stack of 1s from the bottom up to wherever VIN sits; and a priority encoder folds that thermometer into a 3-bit number on D2, D1, D0. No clock, no searching, one step. The cost of that speed is parts: an N-bit flash needs 2^N minus 1 comparators, so this 3-bit part carries seven. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the quantization staircase. The second is the flow analogy of the whole, a measuring column whose fill level is VIN read against seven graduated marks. The third opens that column into the seven comparators drawn as pressure-pilot diff-pair valves, the thermometer visible as a stack of tripped valves feeding the encoder. The fourth is the real device, the full schematic of the ladder, seven comparators, and the encoder gates. The fifth is the silicon, the matched resistor ladder and a comparator diff-pair in cross-section. The staircase scope is the payoff: sweep VIN from 0 to VREF and watch the comparators trip bottom-up while the code steps 0 to 7. The thresholds shown are exact eighths of full scale; the metastable softness at each step edge is widened for the eye.
