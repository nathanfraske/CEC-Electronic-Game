The analog switch, five layers down

An analog switch passes a signal in either direction, turned on and off by a digital control, and it takes both an NMOS and a PMOS to do it well. This is a single SPST CMOS transmission gate, drawn as one switch cell of a CD4066B quad bilateral switch (the CD4066-style part the game models) brought out as a single five-pin part. An NMOS alone passes a strong low but weakens as the signal climbs toward VCC; a PMOS alone passes a strong high but weakens toward GND. Wire them in parallel, drive their gates with complementary controls so both turn on together, and the pair passes the full rail-to-rail signal at a low on-resistance R_on. The first tier is the switch symbol on the real pinout, with CTRL opening and closing it. The second is the gate as a flow network, a gated bidirectional pipe between A and B whose narrowness is R_on. The third opens that into two complementary pilot valves in parallel, one passing well from the high side, one from the low side. The fourth is the real device, the pass NMOS in parallel with the pass PMOS with the control inverter that makes the complementary gate drive, drawn as real transistors. The fifth is the silicon, the two pass devices in cross-section, source and drain symmetric because the signal runs either way. The transfer curve is the on-resistance across the signal swing: each device alone blows up at one rail, and the parallel pair stays low across the whole range.
