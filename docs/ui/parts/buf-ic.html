BUFPULL-UP

The buffer, five layers down

A buffer copies its input straight to its output: high in gives high out, low in gives low out. Logically it changes nothing, so why build a gate for it? Because it restores and drives the signal. This is one gate, the single-buffer chip 74LVC1G34, which takes a weak or slow input and delivers a clean output that can source or sink 24 mA, enough to drive a long trace, fan out to many loads, or translate between 3.3 V and 5 V rails. The trick is that a non-inverting buffer is two inverters in series: the first flips A to NOT A, the second flips it back to A and drives it hard. The first tier is the logic symbol with the real five-pin pinout. The second is the overall analogy, the two stages as a flow network. The third opens the valves as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the real device, two CMOS pairs with a wide output stage. The fifth is the silicon. The input is a real voltage, so the transfer curve shows both the middle inversion and the restored output.
