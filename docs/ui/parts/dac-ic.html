The ladder DAC, five layers down

A resistor ladder turns a binary code into a voltage, and you can point at every resistor doing its job. This is the house CEC1083, a 3-bit digital-to-analog converter built from nothing but an R-2R ladder, and the reconstruct half of the converter pair with the CEC1080 flash ADC. Three input bits, each an ordinary logic level of 0 or VCC, are weighted by powers of two (the MSB counts for 4, the next for 2, the LSB for 1) and summed by the ladder, so the output settles to AOUT = (4 D2 + 2 D1 + D0) / 8 of VCC: eight even steps from 0 to seven eighths of full scale, one LSB = VCC / 8. There is no op-amp, no switches, and no reference pin, since VCC is the reference. The first tier is the symbol on the real pinout with the reconstruction staircase. The second is the flow analogy of the whole, three weighted taps filling one output tank. The third opens that into the R-2R current-halving ladder, where each rung halves a bit's contribution. The fourth is the real device, the exact six-resistor R-2R schematic, one to one with what the simulator builds. The fifth is the silicon, the same resistors as matched on-die strips, since matching, not absolute value, sets the accuracy. The staircase scope is the payoff and the mirror image of the ADC: there a voltage became a code, here a code becomes a voltage. Count up through the eight codes and watch AOUT climb the stairs in equal steps.
