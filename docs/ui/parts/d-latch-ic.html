The transparent D-latch, five layers down

A transparent D-latch stores one bit, but unlike a flip-flop it is level-sensitive. This is the house part CEC3014, a Critical Error gated latch. While the enable EN is high the latch is transparent and the output Q simply follows the data input D; the instant EN goes low the latch freezes the last value of D and holds it. Inside it is the same cross-coupled NOR pair as the SR latch with a small steering front end: an inverter makes NOT D, and two AND gates gated by EN form the set and reset signals, so when EN is low both are low and the latch holds, and when EN is high the latch tracks D. Putting this level-sensitive latch beside an edge-triggered flip-flop is the clearest way to see the difference between level and edge triggering. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the function table. The second is the flow network, the enable gate admitting D into the bistable. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the gate-level schematic, the steering inverter and AND gates feeding the cross-coupled NOR pair, all powered from the rails. The fifth is the silicon, a NOR cell laid out as its CMOS transistors.
