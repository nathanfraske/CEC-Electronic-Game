The NOT gate, five layers down

A NOT gate, or inverter, flips its input: high in gives low out, low in gives high out. Here it is one gate, the single-inverter chip 74LVC1G04, not a row of six. The first tier is the logic symbol with the real five-pin pinout. The second is the overall analogy, the whole inverter as a flow network where a supply tank fills an output chamber through a pull-up valve and a drain empties it through a pull-down valve, both piloted by the input. The third opens those two valves up as pressure-pilot valves, the working MOSFET analogy: a sealed gate line sets a spring-loaded plug, stacked pull-up over pull-down. The fourth is the real device, the SOT-23-5 package with its pinout and the CMOS pair drawn as a schematic. The fifth is the silicon, the metal-oxide cross-section of that pair on one die. The input is a real voltage, so the logic shows at the ends and the crossover shows in the transfer curve.
