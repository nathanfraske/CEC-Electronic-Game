CEC1041SAMPLE

The clocked 1-bit quantizer, five layers down

A quantizer is the instant the analog world becomes a number. This is the CEC1041, a Critical Error Computing house teaching chip: a single clocked 1-bit quantizer, the keystone atom of an analog-to-digital converter, in a five-pin package. On each rising edge of the clock CLK it compares the analog input IN against its internal half-rail reference (one half of VCC) and latches one bit on Q: high if IN sits above the threshold, low if below. Between edges it ignores IN and holds the captured bit. That is SAMPLE, freezing the value on the edge, plus QUANTIZE, deciding which side of the threshold, the two irreducible halves of digitising a signal. The threshold is not a pin; it is set on-chip at half of VCC, so moving VCC moves the decision level with it. The first tier is the symbol with the real pinout, the half-rail threshold marked, and a live timing diagram. The second is the flow network, a sample-and-hold dipper that scoops the input level on the clock tick and compares it to the threshold mark. The third opens that mechanism as pressure-pilot valves, the comparison balance plus the clock-gated capture. The fourth is the schematic, the clocked comparator built from a real NMOS differential pair feeding a regenerative cross-coupled sense latch over a clock-gated tail. The fifth is the silicon. The cross-coupled cell at the heart of the latch is the same regenerative loop that stores a bit everywhere in a chip. Stack eight of these against a resistor ladder and you have a flash converter.
