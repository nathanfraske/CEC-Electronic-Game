The 555 timer, five layers down

The 555 timer turns a ramping voltage into a square wave, which is the whole bridge from the analog world to the digital one. This is the NE555, built around two comparators and a latch. A three-resistor divider sets two thresholds at 1/3 and 2/3 of VCC. An external capacitor charges and discharges between them: one comparator trips when the cap reaches 2/3 VCC, the other when it falls to 1/3 VCC, and an SR latch between them remembers which way the cap is going, driving the output and a discharge switch. The first tier is the symbol with the real pinout, the canonical astable hookup, and the scope. The second is the flow network, the timing cap charging and draining between the two threshold taps. The third opens the comparators as pressure-pilot valves and the latch as a detented mechanism. The fourth is the internal block diagram, the three-resistor ladder, the two comparators, the SR flip-flop, the discharge transistor, and the output stage. The fifth is the silicon, the matched ladder and comparator pairs, with the bipolar and CMOS variants contrasted.
