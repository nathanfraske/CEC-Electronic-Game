The SAR ADC, five layers down

Successive approximation turns a voltage into a number by playing a game of higher or lower. Where the flash ADC asks every level at once, the house CEC1108 makes one comparator do the work over three clocks: guess the top bit, build that guess into a trial voltage with an R-2R DAC, compare it to VIN, and keep the bit if VIN is still higher, drop it if not, then move to the next bit. Three guesses pin a 3-bit code, the same code the CEC1080 flash ADC finds in parallel, reached here with one comparator instead of seven. The first tier is the symbol on the real pinout. The second is the analogy, a balance scale that weighs VIN against trial weights of 4, 2, and 1 dropped in turn, kept or lifted. The third opens that into a hydraulic loop: the DAC as an R-2R ladder of pipes and bit-valves, the comparator as a float balance weighing two standpipe heads, and a clock-driven selector, all plumbed into a feedback ring. The fourth is the real device, that same loop as a block schematic, the comparator and three flip-flops and the CEC1083 ladder. The fifth is the silicon of the three sub-blocks on one die. The convergence scope is the payoff: watch the trial voltage zig-zag in toward VIN by halves while the code builds most-significant-bit first and DONE rises. Move VIN, or press convert, to run the search again.
