The half adder, five layers down

A half adder is the smallest piece of binary arithmetic: it adds two bits and reports a sum and a carry. This is the house part CEC2024, a Critical Error Computing teaching chip that no vendor sells alone, since a one-bit adder has always been an XOR and an AND you wire yourself. The sum bit SUM is A xor B, high when exactly one input is high. The carry bit COUT is A and B, high only when both are high. Together they are the bottom rung of a ripple-carry adder: add a carry-in and the cell becomes the CEC2018 full adder. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the truth table. The second is the flow network, two input streams feeding a sum channel that fills when the inputs differ and a carry channel that fills only when both run. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves, the sum as a differential valve that lifts on the pressure difference between A and B, the carry as a conjunction valve that lifts only when both lines are pressurized. The fourth is the gate-level schematic, the two real gates that are the device: an XOR on A and B making SUM and an AND on A and B making COUT. The fifth is the silicon, a representative gate of the pair laid out as its CMOS transistors on one die.
