The UART, five layers down

Serial is a parallel word sent one bit at a time through a shift register. This is the house CEC5232, an asynchronous serial port, the two-wire link under every serial console and the RS-232 line it is named for. There is no clock wire: both ends agree a baud rate beforehand, and an internal baud counter divides the system tick down to one bit-time. The TX line idles high; on SEND it frames a byte as a START bit (low), then eight data bits least-significant-bit first, then a STOP bit (high), one bit per baud period. The receiver watches RX for the falling START edge, then samples each bit in the middle of its window, so small clock differences do not matter, and pulses RXVALID when the byte lands. Slower than SPI, but it needs no clock wire. The first tier is the logic symbol on the real pinout with the live protocol timing diagram, the signature of a serial part. The second is the flow network, the shift register as a bucket brigade an internal baud metronome marches one bit per tick, wrapped with a START and a STOP. The third opens that as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the real device at the register-transfer level, the shift register as a chain of eight D flip-flops with a baud counter, a framing FSM, and a mid-bit RX sampler, all powered from the rails. The fifth is the silicon, one flip-flop of that register laid out as its CMOS transistors, the cell the whole register repeats.
