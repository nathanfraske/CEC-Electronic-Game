The XNOR gate, five layers down

An XNOR gate gives high out when its two inputs are the same, and low out when they differ. It is the complement of XOR, the equivalence or "is-equal" gate. Here it is one gate in a five-pin package with inputs A and B on pins 1 and 2. There is a real wrinkle: of the seven named two-input gates, XNOR is the one with no dedicated single-gate part in the common 74LVC1G line, so it is realized from a configurable universal gate or a dedicated quad XNOR. The silicon shown is the actual XNOR cell. Like XOR it needs both NOT A and NOT B, so it is two input inverters feeding an and-or-invert core, twelve transistors, with the two hidden nodes Ai and Bi. The one change from XOR is that the core branches are crossed: A pairs with NOT B and NOT A pairs with B, instead of A with B and NOT A with NOT B. That single rewiring computes Y as NOT of (A and Bi, or Ai and B), which flips the gate from high-when-different to high-when-same. The first tier is the logic symbol with the bubble that marks the inversion. The second is the gate as a flow network. The third opens it up as pressure-pilot valves. The fourth is the device as a schematic, the two inverters driving the crossed eight-transistor core. The fifth is the silicon, the metal-oxide cross-section of all twelve devices, drawn here as the pull-up network stacked over the pull-down network. Each input is a real voltage, so the logic shows at the ends and the switching event shows in the transfer curve.
