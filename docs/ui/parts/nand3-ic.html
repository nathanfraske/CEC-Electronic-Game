The 3-input NAND, five layers down

A 3-input NAND gives low out only when all three inputs are high, and high out otherwise. Here it is one gate, the single 3-input NAND chip SN74LVC1G10, in a six-pin package with inputs A, B, and C. It is the 2-input NAND with the stacks grown by one: the pull-down is three NMOS in series, so the output only reaches ground when A and B and C all turn on, and the pull-up is three PMOS in parallel, so any one low input is enough to hold the output high. Six transistors, no hidden nodes, just a wider gate. The first tier is the logic symbol with the real pinout and the eight-row table. The second is the gate as a flow network: a supply tank, three parallel pull-up valves where any one fills the output chamber, and three series pull-down valves where all three must open to drain. The third opens those valves as pressure-pilot valves, the working MOSFET analogy. The fourth is the device as a schematic, three series NMOS under three parallel PMOS. The fifth is the silicon, the metal-oxide cross-section of all six devices on one die. The cost of stacking shows here too: three NMOS in series make a weaker, slower pull-down than one, which is the practical limit on how many inputs a single gate stacks. The transfer curve sweeps input A with B and C held high, where the gate collapses to an inverter on A, so the curve is a falling step.
